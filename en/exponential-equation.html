Mastering Complex Exponential Equations

A step-by-step interactive guide to solving advanced equations with algebraic identities.

Part 1: Foundation - Essential Knowledge

1. Identities $(x \pm a)^2$

Subtraction $(-a)$:

  • $x^2 - 2x + 1 = (x - 1)^2$
  • $x^2 - 4x + 4 = (x - 2)^2$
  • $x^2 - 6x + 9 = (x - 3)^2$

Addition $(+a)$:

  • $x^2 + 2x + 1 = (x + 1)^2$
  • $x^2 + 4x + 4 = (x + 2)^2$
  • $x^2 + 6x + 9 = (x + 3)^2$

2. Exponential Rules

  • $(a^m)^n = a^{m \cdot n}$ (Ex: $(e^2)^3 = e^6$)
  • $a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$ (Ex: $e^2 \cdot e^x = e^{2+x}$)
  • $a^{-m} \cdot a^m = a^0 = 1$ (Ex: $e^{-2} \cdot e^2 = 1$)

Quick Challenges (Quiz)

Type 1: Polynomial Transformation

Q1.1: Transform $x^2 - 4x + 5$ into $(x-a)^2 + k$?

Answer: $x^2 - 4x + 4 + 1 = (x-2)^2 + 1$.

Q1.2: Transform $x^2 + 6x + 10$ into $(x+a)^2 + k$?

Answer: $x^2 + 6x + 9 + 1 = (x+3)^2 + 1$.

Type 2: Power Rules

Q2.1: Simplify $(e^2)^{x^2-4x+5}$?

Answer: $e^{2(x^2-4x+5)} = e^{2(x-2)^2 + 2}$.

Q2.2: Calculate $e^4 \cdot e^{-4}$?

Answer: $e^{4-4} = e^0 = 1$.

Part 2: Detailed Step-by-Step Solution

Original Equation:

$$(1 + e^{-2})(e^2)^{x^2-4x+5} = e^2 + e^{4(x-2)^2}$$

Step 1: Analyze and Simplify Exponents

Objective: Bring exponential expressions to a common intermediate variable form.

Observe the exponent $x^2 - 4x + 5$. We can rewrite it using a perfect square identity:

$$x^2 - 4x + 5 = (x^2 - 4x + 4) + 1 = (x-2)^2 + 1$$

Now, substitute this back into the Left Hand Side (LHS):

$$(1 + e^{-2}) \cdot (e^2)^{(x-2)^2 + 1}$$

Apply the rule $(a^m)^n = a^{m \cdot n}$:

$$(1 + e^{-2}) \cdot e^{2 \cdot [(x-2)^2 + 1]} = (1 + e^{-2}) \cdot e^{2(x-2)^2 + 2}$$

Step 2: Transform the Left-Hand Side (LHS)

Objective: Distribute and simplify the constant $e^{-2}$.

Split the exponent $e^{2(x-2)^2 + 2}$ into $e^{2(x-2)^2} \cdot e^2$, then distribute the term $(1 + e^{-2})$:

$$LHS = (1 + e^{-2}) \cdot e^{2(x-2)^2} \cdot e^2 = e^2 \cdot e^{2(x-2)^2} + (e^{-2} \cdot e^2) \cdot e^{2(x-2)^2}$$

Since $e^{-2} \cdot e^2 = e^0 = 1$, the expression simplifies significantly:

$$LHS = e^2 \cdot e^{2(x-2)^2} + e^{2(x-2)^2}$$

Step 3: Substitution and Equation Setup

Objective: Convert the exponential equation into a quadratic algebraic equation.

Let $t = e^{2(x-2)^2}$. Since $(x-2)^2 \geq 0$, we have $t \geq 1$.

The term $e^{4(x-2)^2}$ on the Right Hand Side (RHS) is equivalent to $t^2$ because $e^{4(x-2)^2} = (e^{2(x-2)^2})^2$.

Substituting into the simplified equation:

$$e^2 \cdot t + t = e^2 + t^2 \Rightarrow t^2 - (e^2 + 1)t + e^2 = 0$$

Step 4: Solve for the Auxiliary Variable $t$

Objective: Use factorization to find the values of $t$.

Group the terms to find common factors:

$$(t^2 - t \cdot e^2) - (t - e^2) = 0 \Rightarrow t(t - e^2) - 1(t - e^2) = 0$$

Factor out the common term $(t - e^2)$:

$$(t - 1)(t - e^2) = 0 \Rightarrow t = 1 \text{ or } t = e^2$$

Both values satisfy $t \geq 1$.

Step 5: Solve for x and Conclusion

Objective: Solve basic exponential equations for the final variable $x$.

Case 1: $t = 1$

$e^{2(x-2)^2} = 1 \Rightarrow x = 2$

Case 2: $t = e^2$

$e^{2(x-2)^2} = e^2 \Rightarrow x = 1, 3$

Final Answer: $x \in \{1, 2, 3\}$

Part 3: Self-Practice Challenges

Answer: $x = 1 \pm \sqrt{2}$.

Answer: $x = -1, 0, -2$.

🚀 Part 4: The Strategy (U-S-F)

To tackle similar problems in the future, remember the "U-S-F" rule:

1. Unify

Standardize complex exponents into a common variable form (usually by using algebraic identities or isolating constants).

2. Substitute

Use a temporary variable $t$ to transform a "bulky" exponential equation into a familiar quadratic or cubic algebraic equation.

3. Factorize

Always prioritize grouping and factoring over calculating irrational numbers like $e$ or $\pi$ too early.
